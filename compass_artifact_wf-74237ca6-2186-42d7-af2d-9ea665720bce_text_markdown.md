# The complete guide to Italian factoring and invoice finance products

**Italy's €289 billion factoring market — the fourth-largest in Europe and representing ~13% of GDP — offers the most diverse ecosystem of receivables finance products on the continent.** Non-recourse factoring dominates at 81% of turnover, but the market spans 16 distinct product categories from traditional factoring to B2B BNPL, each shaped by Italy's unique combination of chronic late payments (~140-day averages), mandatory e-invoicing infrastructure, and a massive public administration supply chain. Factoring advances now account for **over 40% of all short-term corporate loans** in Italy, doubling their share over the past decade. This report examines every product available in the market, covering mechanics, pricing, providers, and legal foundations.

---

## 1. Factoring pro soluto dominates Italy with 81% market share

**Italian legal term:** *Cessione del credito pro soluto* / *cessione senza garanzia di solvenza*
**Legal basis:** L. 52/1991 Art. 4 (factor's express waiver of solvency guarantee); Arts. 1260–1267 Codice Civile; Art. 106 TUB (D.Lgs. 385/1993)

Non-recourse factoring transfers the debtor's credit risk entirely to the factor. In 2024, pro soluto generated **€232.7 billion in turnover**, growing 3.1% year-on-year while recourse factoring declined.

**Step-by-step mechanics.** The seller (*cedente*) and factor (*cessionario*) execute a master factoring agreement (*contratto di factoring con clausola pro soluto*). The factor evaluates each debtor (*debitore ceduto*) and sets revolving credit limits (*plafond*) per debtor. The seller assigns commercial receivables, transmitting invoices and supporting documents. Typically, the debtor is notified of the assignment (*cessione notificata*), though non-notification variants exist. The factor advances **up to 80% of nominal receivable value** — ranging 70–90% across the market, with SACE Fct going up to 100% for certified PA receivables. The factor assumes 100% insolvency risk within the plafond. If the debtor defaults, the factor pays a guarantee amount (*pagamento in garanzia*) after a contractual waiting period of **150–240 days** post-maturity.

**Pricing structure.** Three components drive the cost. First, a factoring commission (*commissione di factoring*): a flat percentage on invoice face value, typically **0.3%–2.0%** for standard operations (ceiling rates of 4.5–5.0% per published *fogli informativi*). Second, interest on advances: variable rate linked to **Euribor + 2%–5% spread** (maximum nominal annual rates of 6.8–9.5% per published schedules). Third, handling fees of €1–10 per invoice and a plus-factoring commission on overdue receivables. All rates are subject to TEGM usury limits under L. 108/1996. Minimum annual commissions of €5,000–25,000 are common.

**Why pro soluto dominates.** The 81% share reflects several structural forces. Italian companies need balance sheet optimization: pro soluto contracts structured as *acquisto a titolo definitivo* (ATD) meet IFRS 9 paragraph 3.2.6(a) derecognition criteria, allowing companies to remove receivables entirely from their balance sheet. Italy's chronically long payment times make risk transfer essential — over 30% of factoring portfolios are PA receivables where payment delays are endemic. SMEs are particularly sensitive to credit risk, and the SACE *Garanzia Italia* extended to pro soluto operations provides additional state-backed incentives.

**Accounting treatment.** Standard pro soluto may not always qualify for IFRS 9 derecognition if the seller retains dilution risk or contractual compliance obligations. A specialized "Pro Soluto IAS/ATD" variant — offered by Société Générale Factoring, Banca MPS, and Intesa Sanpaolo — is specifically engineered so that substantially all risks and benefits transfer, meeting the derecognition test. The IFRS 9 analysis compares pre- and post-transfer variability of the present value of future net cash flows.

**Key providers:** UniCredit Factoring (~€50 billion turnover), Intesa Sanpaolo/Factorit (~€44 billion), Ifitalia/BNP Paribas (~€18 billion), Banca Ifis (~€7.8 billion), SACE Fct (~€3.5 billion), MBFacta, Crédit Agricole Factoring, BPER Factor, Sarda Factoring, Credemfactor.

**E-invoicing interaction.** Since January 2019, all B2B invoices transit through the *Sistema di Interscambio* (SDI) in XML *FatturaPA* format, providing factors with real-time, government-validated invoice data. This enables automated verification of invoice authenticity, eliminates duplicate financing fraud risk, and powers the Assifact FactDesk project's anti-double-financing register.

**Key risks.** For the factor: debtor insolvency within plafond, dilution risk (credit notes, disputes), and fraud by the seller (non-existent invoices). For the seller: breach of representations and warranties (*garanzia di esistenza, certezza, liquidità, esigibilità*) can trigger loss of pro soluto protection and an obligation to repurchase. Contract duration is typically indefinite (*a tempo indeterminato*) with free withdrawal per TUB Art. 118, though future credit assignments are limited to a 24-month window under L. 52/1991 Art. 3.

## 2. Factoring pro solvendo serves cost-sensitive borrowers

**Italian legal term:** *Cessione del credito pro solvendo* / *cessione con garanzia di solvenza*
**Legal basis:** L. 52/1991 Art. 4 (default solvency guarantee); Art. 1267 Codice Civile

Recourse factoring accounts for **€55.8 billion (19% of turnover)** and has been in structural decline, falling 5.1% in 2024 as firms shift toward pro soluto for balance sheet optimization.

**How it differs mechanically.** The factor does not assume insolvency risk. The seller retains the *obbligo di regresso* (recourse obligation) — a contingent liability. The factor provides financing through advances, receivables management, and collection services, but if the debtor fails to pay at maturity for any reason, the factor "puts back" the receivable: the seller must repay the advance plus accrued interest, commissions, and expenses. Under Art. 1267 Codice Civile, the guarantee ceases if the factor was negligent in pursuing collection.

**Pricing advantage.** Pro solvendo is cheaper because there is no credit risk premium. The factoring commission drops to **0.2%–1.5%** (versus 0.5%–5.0% for pro soluto), and there is no separate guarantee/risk commission. Interest on advances is typically **Euribor + 1.5%–4%** — modestly below pro soluto rates. Faster processing is also possible since less debtor due diligence is needed.

**Balance sheet treatment.** Receivables generally **remain on the seller's balance sheet** because IFRS 9 derecognition criteria are not met — the seller retains insolvency risk through the recourse obligation. The advance is recorded as a liability; the receivable stays as an asset. Under Italian GAAP (OIC 15), the treatment follows the same logic.

**Typical use cases.** Companies with high-quality debtors (blue-chip corporations, PA entities) where insolvency risk is minimal and the primary need is liquidity. Also preferred when companies hold independent credit insurance covering their receivables, or when cost sensitivity is paramount. The savings of 0.3%–2% in commission versus pro soluto can be material on large portfolios.

**Key risks.** For the seller: full recourse means repayment obligation if the debtor defaults, creating potential cash flow strain if multiple debtors fail simultaneously. For the factor: dual default risk — both the debtor and the seller may become insolvent, leaving the factor unable to recover.

## 3. Maturity factoring provides credit protection without financing

**Italian legal term:** *Factoring a scadenza*
**Legal basis:** L. 52/1991; Arts. 1260 et seq. Codice Civile

Maturity factoring reached **€55.8 billion (+8.8% year-on-year)** in 2024, representing a significant portion of the market. It operates without advance financing — the factor pays the seller at the invoice maturity date, not before.

**Mechanics.** The seller assigns receivables to the factor, which manages the entire lifecycle: registration, accounting, monitoring, dunning, and collection. No advance payment is made. At the invoice maturity date (*data di scadenza*), the factor pays the seller the full nominal value. Three variants exist. In maturity pro soluto, the factor guarantees payment at maturity regardless of whether the debtor has actually paid — absorbing both credit risk and timing risk. In maturity pro solvendo, the factor pays only upon actual collection from the debtor. In the maturity pro soluto ATD variant, payment occurs at the maturity date itself, provided debtor acknowledgment exists.

**The maturity guarantee concept.** When combined with pro soluto, this product offers a powerful guarantee: the seller receives payment on a predetermined date even if the debtor is late or insolvent. If the debtor is late, the factor may grant the debtor a *dilazione* (extension) and charge interest directly to the debtor for the extension period.

**Pricing.** Since there is no advance, there is no interest charge — the cost is **commission only** (0.3%–2.0% on nominal value). If the seller subsequently requests advances, interest is charged, transforming the arrangement partially into a credit-cash factoring product.

**Who uses it.** Companies that don't need financing but want credit protection and payment certainty — particularly large companies with PA debtors who pay late but are ultimately solvent. The product originated in Italy's healthcare sector, where private clinics dealing with NHS (*Servizio Sanitario Nazionale*) entities needed payment certainty from slow but sovereign-backed debtors.

**Key providers:** Banca Sistema (specialist), SACE Fct, MBFacta, BPER Factor, Sarda Factoring, Ifitalia, Intesa Sanpaolo.

## 4. Reverse factoring transforms supplier finance through buyer programs

**Italian legal term:** *Factoring indiretto* / *Confirming*
**Legal basis:** L. 52/1991 (atypical contract, confirmed by Cassazione 2746/2007); TUB Art. 106; IFRS Interpretations Committee guidance (June 2020)

Reverse factoring is the fastest-evolving segment. SCF via reverse factoring/confirming reached **€28 billion (+0.9%)** in 2024, with confirming programs growing **6.2%** to €4.2 billion.

**Step-by-step mechanics.** A large buyer (*impresa focale*) signs a framework agreement (*convenzione*) with a factor. The buyer identifies strategic suppliers and the factor onboards them through KYC/AML processes. When a supplier delivers goods and issues an e-invoice via SDI, the buyer validates and "confirms" the invoice on the factor's platform, committing to pay at maturity. The supplier cedes the confirmed receivable to the factor (pro soluto or pro solvendo) and receives early payment — the invoice amount minus a discount. At the original or extended due date, the buyer pays the full invoice amount to the factor.

**The credit arbitrage at the heart of pricing.** The critical advantage is that **supplier financing is priced based on the buyer's creditworthiness**, not the supplier's. An investment-grade buyer enables its SME suppliers to access financing at spreads of **Euribor + 1%–3% annualized** — far below what those SMEs could obtain independently. The buyer typically pays a platform/program fee and may negotiate to extend payment terms (from 60 to 90 or 120 days).

**Major Italian programs.** Fincantieri launched an ESG-linked reverse factoring program in March 2023 with Crédit Agricole Eurofactor, Ifitalia, SACE Fct, and UniCredit, where discount rates are linked to suppliers' ESG ratings on an A–E scale. ENI's "Sustainable Supply Chain Finance" program, also launched March 2023, is powered by FinDynamic's platform, funded by Crédit Agricole and UniCredit, and links conditions to ESG performance via ENI's Open-es platform. Enel runs major SCF programs leveraging its investment-grade rating across its global supply chain.

**Italian confirming versus Spanish confirming.** The term *Confirming* originated in Spain in the early 1990s (originally a Santander trademark). Spanish confirming functions as a mass payment management system — open and democratic, representing ~50% of Spain's total factoring/confirming volumes (€100+ billion). Italian confirming is more targeted, focused on strategic supplier financing rather than mass payments, with higher financing utilization (~100% of onboarded suppliers versus ~50% in Spain) and increasingly integrated with ESG criteria. Spain maintains a ~50/50 split between factoring and confirming; Italy's SCF represents roughly 10% of total factoring.

**Key IFRS risk.** The IFRS Interpretations Committee (June 2020) provided guidance that reverse factoring liabilities should be classified as trade payables unless their nature warrants separate disclosure as financial liabilities. Rating agencies (Fitch, Moody's, S&P) increasingly scrutinize reverse factoring for potential hidden debt — extended payment terms can mask financial stress, and reclassification from trade payables to financial debt worsens leverage ratios.

**Key providers:** Intesa Sanpaolo, UniCredit Factoring, Ifitalia/BNP Paribas, SACE Fct, Crédit Agricole Eurofactor, BPER Factor, Banca MPS, Generalfinance.

## 5. Supply chain finance encompasses a €131 billion Italian ecosystem

**Legal basis:** No dedicated SCF legislation; individual instruments governed by respective laws

The Politecnico di Milano Observatory on Supply Chain Finance (established 2013) defines SCF as "the set of models, solutions and services that can be used to optimise financial performance — especially in terms of controlling working capital — and which exploit knowledge gained from managing supply chain relationships."

**Observatory taxonomy and market sizing (2024 data, presented March 2025).** The total addressable market is ~€596 billion. Total served by SCF solutions reached **€131 billion**, representing **22% of the potential market**. The breakdown: traditional factoring (€60.4 billion), invoice advance/*anticipo fattura* (€54 billion), reverse factoring (€9 billion), B2B credit card (€3.8 billion), confirming (€1.6 billion per Politecnico; €4.2 billion per Assifact's broader definition), purchase order finance (€1.4 billion, **+35% year-on-year**), dynamic discounting (€0.8 billion, **+17%**), and invoice trading (€0.6 billion, +5%).

**Key Italian platforms.** FinDynamic (Milan, founded circa 2016 from a Politecnico di Milano study) is Italy's leading SCF fintech, offering dynamic discounting and confirming on a multi-product, multi-bank platform integrated with 400+ ERP/TMS systems. Other platforms include CashMe, PrimeRevenue, C2FO, Workinvoice, PlusAdvance, Bflows, Piteco, and Kyriba. Multi-funder architecture — where multiple banks compete to fund supplier payments through a single platform — is increasingly standard, as demonstrated by ENI's dual-funded program with Crédit Agricole and UniCredit via FinDynamic.

**The 12th edition Observatory (2024/2025)** was supported by ~30 partners including Accenture, Allianz Trade, American Express, Banca Sella, FinDynamic, Intesa Sanpaolo, PrimeRevenue, and Assifact. Key themes include GenAI in SCF, EU CSRD sustainability reporting integration, and ESG-linked supply chain finance.

## 6. Dynamic discounting lets cash-rich buyers earn risk-free returns

**Italian legal term:** *Sconto dinamico*
**Legal basis:** General commercial law (no specific regulation); buyer-supplier payment terms under contract law

Dynamic discounting is a buyer-funded program where the buyer uses its own excess cash — not bank financing — to pay suppliers early in exchange for a sliding-scale discount. No third-party financing is involved. The market reached **€0.8 billion in 2024, growing 17% year-on-year**.

**Sliding-scale mechanism.** Unlike static discounts (e.g., "2/10 net 30"), the discount is calculated continuously based on the number of days of early payment. On 90-day standard terms, payment at day 10 (80 days early) might yield a ~2.4% discount (equivalent to ~11% APR), while payment at day 60 (30 days early) yields ~0.9%. Buyers typically target **8–15% annualized return** on deployed cash, which translates to daily rates of approximately 0.02–0.04% per day of acceleration. For suppliers, the effective cost of **3–8% APR** is far below bank overdraft rates of 10–20%+ that many Italian SMEs face.

**Critical differences from reverse factoring.** Dynamic discounting involves no bank, creates no additional debt, triggers no IFRS reclassification risk, requires no supplier KYC/AML with banks (dramatically simplifying onboarding), and is limited only by the buyer's available cash. It is ideal for the "long tail" of smaller suppliers who don't qualify for bank reverse factoring programs — banks typically only underwrite the top 15–20% of a buyer's supplier base.

**FinDynamic's implementation.** The company integrates automatically with client ERPs (400+ compatible systems), allowing buyers to define discount rate curves per supplier category. Suppliers receive notifications, view eligible invoices, and choose which to accelerate. Payment happens next business day from the buyer's own accounts. Key Italian clients include ENI, Cisalfa Sport Group (€15 million UniCredit/FinDynamic transaction), Venchi, and Unicomm. FinDynamic's platform volumes nearly doubled between 2022 and 2024 (index: 100 → 198), earning it a spot in the Financial Times Top 1000 for growth.

**E-invoicing integration.** Credemtel's GAWDD service connects its e-invoicing platform (GAWFE) directly to FinDynamic, creating a seamless e-invoice-to-dynamic-discounting workflow. Invoice data flows automatically from ERP → SDI → platform → supplier notification → payment execution → accounting reconciliation.

## 7. Invoice trading platforms bring capital markets into SME receivables

**Italian legal term:** *Cessione del credito* via piattaforme digitali
**Legal basis:** Arts. 1260 et seq. Codice Civile (general assignment of receivables); L. 130/1999 (for SPV investors); AIFMD (for fund investors)

Invoice trading platforms — online marketplaces where SMEs sell individual invoices to institutional investors — generated **~€0.6 billion in 2024 (+5%)**, a small but fast-growing niche with historical CAGRs exceeding 40%.

**Mechanics.** An SME (*società di capitali*) registers on a platform and uploads digital invoices (XML from SDI). The platform uses Big Data, AI, and external data (CRIBIS/D&B credit ratings) to score both seller and debtor. Invoices are listed via auction (Workinvoice, CashMe) or matched with investors through suggested pricing. A *cessione del credito* notification is sent to the debtor. Investors advance **85–90% of face value** (up to 95%) within **24–72 hours**. When the debtor pays at maturity, the balance is settled net of fees.

**Key Italian platforms.** Workinvoice (Italy's first, founded 2013 by Matteo Tarroni, operative from 2015; partnered with CRIBIS/CRIF Group, Banca Sella, Azimut, Enel X; minimum invoice €10,000) operates an auction mechanism. CashMe (part of Gruppo Finservice; over €1 billion cumulative; minimum invoice €5,000) offers pro-soluto auction/marketplace. CashInvoice (founded by Luca Scali; €880 million originated in 2023) operates as a comparator connecting sellers with multiple investor types. Anticipay (part of Gruppo Zucchetti) integrates invoice trading into Zucchetti's accounting software ecosystem. Credimi (now part of Banca CF+) was the only platform registered as Art. 106 TUB intermediary, purchasing invoices directly and securitizing them — but the balance-sheet-intensive model proved unsustainable, and the business was acquired by Banca CF+ in August 2023 for €5.5 million.

**Pricing.** Platform commissions range from **0.4% to 0.9%** of invoice value (Workinvoice: 0.40% for ≤60 days, 0.65% for 61–90 days, 0.90% for >90 days). Investor returns add 1–2%. Total all-in cost to the seller: **1.5–3%** of invoice value depending on debtor quality and tenor.

**Regulatory status.** Most platforms do not need Art. 106 TUB registration because they act as marketplace operators, not lenders — the underlying transaction is a *cessione del credito* (general civil law assignment), not financing activity. Investors may be securitization SPVs (L. 130/1999), alternative investment funds (AIFMD), or non-financial corporates. Crucially, invoice trading transactions do **not trigger Centrale dei Rischi reporting** (unlike factoring/bank credit), which is a significant attraction for SMEs.

**How e-invoicing enables it.** Italy's mandatory e-invoicing provides the foundational data layer: every invoice is a verified, government-validated XML file, enabling instant authenticity verification, preventing double-pledging, and supporting more accurate real-time credit scoring. The ItaliaFintech Position Paper (February 2023) explicitly notes that invoice trading invoices are typically "fatture digitali in formato XML che transitano dal SDI."

**Key risks.** Anti-cession clauses (*clausole di incedibilità*) in buyer contracts remain a major obstacle — ItaliaFintech advocates legislative nullification of these clauses. Scale limitations persist: €0.6 billion versus €60 billion for traditional factoring. Credimi's collapse demonstrated the fragility of balance-sheet-intensive models.

## 8. Forfaiting enables Italian capital goods exports without recourse

**Italian legal term:** *Smobilizzo pro-soluto di crediti a medio-lungo termine*
**Legal basis:** No specific Italian legislation; Art. 1 TUB recognizes forfaiting as *credito commerciale*; L. 227/1977 (*Legge Ossola*) and D.Lgs. 143/1998 for export credit subsidies; ICC URF 800 (Uniform Rules for Forfaiting, 2013)

Forfaiting is the without-recourse purchase of medium-to-long-term trade receivables (**6 months to 7+ years**), incorporated in negotiable instruments, at a discount. It serves Italian exporters of capital goods, machinery, industrial plants, and infrastructure.

**Step-by-step mechanics.** The Italian exporter contacts a forfaiter to assess feasibility and obtain indicative discount rates. The forfaiter issues an irrevocable commitment to discount instruments at agreed terms; the exporter pays a commitment fee. The exporter and foreign importer execute the supply contract requiring payment via promissory notes (*pagherò cambiari*) or bills of exchange (*cambiali tratte*). After shipment, the importer issues or accepts the instruments. The importer's bank provides an *avallo* (aval/guarantee) on the instruments. The exporter endorses instruments to the forfaiter with a "without recourse" / *senza garanzia* clause. The forfaiter discounts the instruments and credits net proceeds. At maturity, the forfaiter presents instruments to the importer's bank for payment. The forfaiter may sell instruments on the secondary market.

**SACE's role.** SACE's *Credito Fornitore* (supplier credit) policy insures the exporter's medium/long-term receivables against political and commercial risk. The policy can be transferred (*volturata*) from the exporter to the forfaiter through a formal transfer process, enabling the forfaiter to become the insured party. SIMEST (part of the SACE group) provides interest equalization subsidies (*contributo in conto interessi*) under D.Lgs. 143/1998, reducing the effective cost for Italian exporters.

**Pricing.** The discount rate combines the cost of funds (EURIBOR-based), a spread based on country risk, tenor, and guarantor bank creditworthiness, plus a commitment fee and handling fee. The aval/guarantee cost is typically borne by the importer.

**Key distinction from export factoring.** Forfaiting deals with medium/long-term negotiable instruments in single transactions, always without recourse. Export factoring involves short-term open-account invoices in revolving facilities and can be with or without recourse. Forfaiting serves machinery, infrastructure, and defense exports; factoring serves ongoing trade in consumer and intermediate goods.

**Italian market context.** Italian banks typically serve as intermediaries finding specialized forfaiters (usually in London, Frankfurt, or Zurich) rather than acting as direct forfaiters. Primary users are machinery and capital goods exporters concentrated in Emilia-Romagna, Lombardy, and Veneto.

## 9. Full-service factoring bundles finance with credit management

**Italian legal term:** *Factoring full service* / *gestione del credito*
**Legal basis:** L. 52/1991; general contract law (ancillary services not specifically regulated)

Full-service factoring integrates five functions into a single contract: **ledger management** (*gestione del partitario*), **dunning** (*solleciti*), **credit assessment** (*valutazione del merito creditizio*), **collection services**, and **financing/guarantee**. Assifact notes that factors possess an "ineguagliabile patrimonio informativo" (unmatched informational heritage) regarding debtor payment behavior.

**How it operates.** The factor maintains the complete receivables sub-ledger on behalf of the seller. All invoices, credit notes, and payments are recorded in the factor's systems, with periodic statements sent to sellers and debtors. The factor monitors payment deadlines, issues automated and personalized payment reminders, and pursues judicial or extrajudicial recovery when needed. Credit assessment includes analyzing financial statements, payment behavior databases, and commercial information to set debtor-specific credit limits.

**Full service versus bulk/selective.** Full-service factoring typically includes a *clausola di globalità* (globality clause) requiring the seller to assign all receivables from designated debtors, plus a *clausola di esclusiva* (exclusivity clause) prohibiting use of competing factors. This differs from selective or "bulk" factoring where only specific invoices or debtors are assigned.

**A management-only variant** (*sola gestione*) exists where the factor provides only administration services without financing or guarantee. Ifitalia describes this as a service "che si caratterizza per la prevalenza del servizio di gestione crediti ceduti," focusing on registration, accounting, monitoring, and reporting.

**Value beyond financing.** Full-service converts fixed internal credit management costs into variable commission-based costs, provides professional expertise in complex collection scenarios, reduces DSO even in pro solvendo arrangements, scales automatically with business growth, and delivers market intelligence on debtor behavior and industry payment trends.

**Key providers:** Ifitalia/BNL-BNP Paribas (historically strong in full-service), Sarda Factoring, Credemfactor, Clessidra Factoring, UniCredit Factoring.

## 10. Confidential factoring now exceeds 50% of Italian turnover

**Italian legal term:** *Factoring non notificato* / *cessione non notificata*
**Legal basis:** Arts. 1260, 1264, 1265 Codice Civile; L. 52/1991 Art. 5

In a striking market feature, **over 50% of Italian factoring turnover is conducted without debtor notification** (Assifact Q1 2024 data), growing 10.4% year-on-year — making confidential factoring the dominant operational mode by volume.

**How it works.** The seller assigns receivables to the factor via the standard factoring contract, but no notification is sent to the debtor. The factor grants the seller a *mandato senza rappresentanza* (mandate without representation) to collect receivables on the factor's behalf. The seller continues managing the customer relationship and collecting payments as the *creditore apparente* (apparent creditor). The debtor pays the seller into a designated *conto corrente vincolato* (restricted bank account). The seller transfers collected funds to the factor, which monitors flows and reconciles payments.

**Legal framework.** Under Art. 1260 Codice Civile, assignment is valid without debtor consent — it is perfected by agreement between seller and factor alone. Art. 1264 provides that without notification, the debtor is validly discharged by paying the original creditor in good faith. For third-party priority, Art. 1265 gives precedence to the first-notified assignment. However, L. 52/1991 Art. 5 provides an alternative enforceability mechanism: if the factor has paid consideration with *data certa* (certain date), the assignment is enforceable against third parties even without notification.

**When preferred.** Companies that don't want customers to perceive factoring use as a sign of financial weakness. Ifitalia explicitly offers non-notification service as "particularly suitable for companies interested in hedging against insolvency risk and that prefer to maintain control of the entire customer portfolio management process." Large corporates with strong internal collection capabilities are primary users.

**Key risks for the factor.** Payment diversion (*decanalizzazione dei flussi*) — the seller may redirect debtor payments away from the designated account. If the seller enters insolvency, collected funds may become part of the insolvency estate. Double assignment risk is elevated since the factor cannot rely on notification-based priority. Mitigation tools include restricted bank accounts, non-possessory chattel pledges (*pegno mobiliare non possessorio*), and the factor's retained right to notify at any time as a safeguard. SDI e-invoice data provides an independent verification layer — particularly valuable when the factor has no direct debtor contact.

## 11. International factoring relies on the FCI two-factor system

**Italian legal term:** *Factoring internazionale* (export/import)
**Legal basis:** L. 52/1991; UNIDROIT Convention on International Factoring (Ottawa 1988, ratified by Italy 25 October 1994); Rome I Regulation (EU 593/2008) Art. 14; FCI General Rules for International Factoring (GRIF)

International factoring grew tenfold over 15 years, rising from 7% to **18% of all Italian factoring activity**. International turnover reached **€72.8 billion in 2024, growing 13.8% year-on-year**, and surged a further 20% in Q1 2025.

**Export factoring from Italy — step by step.** An Italian exporter sells goods on open account to a foreign buyer. The exporter assigns the foreign receivable to an Italian export factor (e.g., UniCredit Factoring, Ifitalia). The Italian factor requests credit limit approval from a correspondent import factor in the buyer's country via FCI's Edifactoring platform. The import factor assesses the foreign buyer and approves a credit limit. Upon shipment, the Italian exporter submits invoices to the export factor, which advances **70–90% of invoice value**. The import factor guarantees payment, manages disputes, and collects from the buyer in local currency. At maturity, funds flow from debtor → import factor → export factor → Italian exporter (less fees).

**Import factoring into Italy.** A foreign exporter assigns Italian receivables to its own export factor, which contacts an Italian import factor. The Italian factor evaluates the Italian buyer, provides a credit guarantee, and manages collection in Italian language and according to Italian commercial practices.

**FCI's infrastructure.** FCI (Amsterdam) operates the global institutional framework with nearly 400 members across 90+ countries, providing the GRIF rules, Edifactoring messaging platform, dispute resolution mechanisms, and FCIreverse (a cross-border reverse factoring platform). UniCredit Factoring won FCI's Best Export & Import Factor of the Year at FCI's 53rd meeting in 2021, three consecutive years running.

**Italy and the UNIDROIT Convention.** Italy was among the first three ratifying states (alongside France and Nigeria) of the 1988 Ottawa Convention, which entered into force on 1 May 1995. The Convention validates assignment of future receivables, authorizes bulk assignments, and overcomes anti-assignment clauses in underlying contracts. UNIDROIT itself is headquartered in Rome, reflecting Italy's institutional role.

**Key Italian factors in international operations:** UniCredit Factoring (global coverage through UniCredit Group and FCI network), Ifitalia/BNP Paribas (international reach through 17-country BNP Paribas Factoring Group network), Banca Ifis (FCI member since 2002, subsidiaries in Romania and Poland), SACE Fct (focus on Italian exporters and PA suppliers), and Société Générale Factoring Italian Branch.

**Pricing.** International factoring carries dual-factor fees — the import factor typically charges **0.3–1.5% commission** on guaranteed amounts in addition to the export factor's charges. Total costs are higher than domestic factoring but lower than letter of credit arrangements.

## 12. PA receivable factoring exploits sovereign safety and endemic delays

**Italian legal term:** *Cessione dei crediti verso la Pubblica Amministrazione*
**Legal basis:** Arts. 1260 et seq. Codice Civile; L. 52/1991; D.Lgs. 50/2016 Art. 106 comma 13 (now D.Lgs. 36/2023 Art. 120 comma 12); R.D. 2440/1923 Arts. 69–70 (State); D.Lgs. 231/2002 (Late Payment Directive); D.L. 66/2014 (credit certification)

PA receivable factoring totaled **€21.69 billion in turnover** in 2024, with **€7.68 billion outstanding** as of December 2025 — of which **€3.1 billion was overdue**. This segment epitomizes the Italian factoring market's unique character: sovereign-grade credit quality combined with endemic payment delays.

**Step-by-step mechanics.** A supplier delivers goods/services and issues an invoice in *FatturaPA* format via SDI (mandatory since 2014–2015 for all PA invoices). SDI automatically forwards invoice data to the *Piattaforma dei Crediti Commerciali* (PCC). The supplier assigns the receivable to a factor, and formal notification must be sent to the PA debtor. The legal complexity depends on the PA type: for State Administration, explicit consent (*assenso*) is required under R.D. 2440/1923. For public contracts, a *silenzio-assenso* (tacit consent) mechanism applies — the PA has 45 days from notification to expressly refuse; absent refusal, the assignment is deemed accepted (Art. 106 comma 13, D.Lgs. 50/2016, now Art. 120 comma 12, D.Lgs. 36/2023). The factor advances **70–90% of invoice value** and manages collection, including pursuing payment injunctions (*ingiunzioni di pagamento*) and late payment interest (*interessi di mora*) under D.Lgs. 231/2002.

**The PCC platform.** Created in 2012 by the *Ragioneria Generale dello Stato* (MEF) and operated by Sogei, the PCC automatically acquires all electronic PA invoices from SDI, monitors the entire invoice lifecycle (received, verified, accounted, suspended, assigned, offset, paid), processes credit certification requests, and tracks assignments. PAs are obligated to update PCC, and the system is fed through SIOPE+ (the dematerialized payment order system).

**Credit certification** (*certificazione del credito*). Creditors submit a request through PCC; the PA has **30 days** to certify that the credit is *certo, liquido ed esigibile* (certain, liquid, and due). Certified credits can be assigned to factors, offset against tax debts (*cartelle esattoriali*), or used to obtain DURC labor compliance certificates. This certification is the only legally recognized official instrument for PA credit verification.

**Payment times — the persistent Italian problem.** Despite improvement (MEF data shows the weighted average dropping from 53 to 29 days for central administrations between 2019 and 2024), significant disparities persist. Healthcare payment times remain at **35 days** on MEF's optimistic measure, but independent analyses suggest substantial underestimation — ANCE (construction industry) found 59% of companies still experienced payment delays in March 2024, and Calabria healthcare was at **245 days** in 2021. Italy was declared non-compliant by the EU Court of Justice in January 2020 (Case C-122/18) for persistent PA payment delays.

**Late payment interest.** Under D.Lgs. 231/2002 (implementing EU Directive 2011/7/EU), interest accrues automatically from the day after the payment deadline at **ECB reference rate + 8 percentage points** (~11–12%), plus a €40 minimum recovery cost — without requiring formal notice. In practice, most suppliers do not claim these interest charges for fear of losing future contracts, creating a unique profit opportunity for specialist factors.

**Why Banca Sistema specializes.** Founded in 2011 during the sovereign debt crisis specifically to support PA suppliers, approximately **70% of its total assets** are factoring exposure to PA receivables. Its competitive advantage lies in a network of local collectors and deep specialization in PA processes. Banca Sistema is now being acquired by Banca CF+ (controlled by Apollo fund), creating a dominant combined entity.

**Why BFF Bank specializes.** Founded in 1985 by a consortium of pharmaceutical companies, BFF Bank operates a pan-European platform across 9 countries. Its unique revenue model aggressively pursues late payment interest through judicial proceedings (*decreti ingiuntivi*) — off-balance-sheet LPI and recovery cost reserves amounted to **€548 million** at end Q1 2023. Major banks like Intesa and UniCredit cannot pursue LPIs this aggressively because they also serve as treasury banks for the same PA entities, creating a conflict. BFF's 2025 adjusted net profit was **€151.7 million** with adjusted ROE of ~23%, on a loan book of €5.8 billion.

**PA receivables and Basel capital rules.** Under the 2021 EU Default Definition, credits overdue >90 days must be classified as defaulted. Since PA frequently pays late, Banca d'Italia forced BFF Bank and Banca Sistema to reclassify large portions of PA credits — despite near-certain recovery. The two specialist banks held **one-third of all overdue credits** in the entire Italian banking system after reclassification, creating capital strain and dividend blocks in 2024–2025.

## 13. Tax credit factoring generated €219 billion before regulatory shutdown

**Italian legal term:** *Cessione dei crediti fiscali* / *cessione del credito d'imposta*
**Legal basis:** Art. 121, D.L. 34/2020 (*Decreto Rilancio*); D.L. 11/2023 (*Decreto Blocca Cessioni*); D.L. 39/2024 (*DL Superbonus*)

Tax credit factoring emerged as a massive factoring-adjacent product from 2020–2023, driven by the **Superbonus 110%** and related construction bonus schemes. Total credits transferred or discounted from October 2020 to April 2024 reached approximately **€219.5 billion** — an extraordinary figure that substantially inflated Italian factoring statistics.

**The mechanism.** The Superbonus 110% (Art. 119, D.L. 34/2020) allowed a 110% tax deduction for qualifying energy efficiency and seismic improvement works on residential buildings. Art. 121 enabled two alternatives to direct deduction: *sconto in fattura* (the contractor applies a discount directly on the invoice and acquires a transferable tax credit) or *cessione del credito* (the beneficiary transfers the tax credit to a third party at a discount). The buyer — typically a bank or financial intermediary — accepts the credit in its *cassetto fiscale* (tax drawer) and uses it to offset own tax liabilities via F24 over 4, 5, or 10 annual installments.

**Pricing.** Poste Italiane, a major buyer, priced Superbonus 110% credits (4-year recovery) at **€94 per €110 nominal** (85.5% of face value). Other bonuses with 5-year recovery: €84.50 per €100 (84.5%); 10-year recovery: €70 per €100 (70%). Implied annual discount rates of roughly 3–5%. Poste imposed caps of €50,000 per transaction and €150,000 cumulative per client.

**The fraud crisis.** The *Guardia di Finanza* seized **€9.3 billion in fake tax credits** (approximately 4% of total transferred credits). Contrary to political narratives, Superbonus 110% accounted for only **3% of fraud cases** (~€360 million); the *Bonus Facciate* was responsible for **51% of all fraud** (€4.2 billion). Fraud mechanisms included shell companies (*cartiere*), fake invoices for works never performed, falsified technical certifications, and chains of fraudulent transfers.

**Regulatory shutdown.** D.L. 11/2023 (*Decreto Blocca Cessioni*), effective 17 February 2023, imposed a general prohibition on new *cessione del credito* and *sconto in fattura* for all *bonus edilizi*, with narrow exceptions for works where permits were filed before the cutoff date. D.L. 39/2024 eliminated most remaining exceptions and, from 2025, prohibited banks/intermediaries from compensating cessione-derived tax credits via F24. The Superbonus rate was reduced from 110% (2020–2022) to 90% (2023), 70% (2024), and 65% (2025), with no renewal planned for 2026 except in earthquake zones.

**Impact on factoring statistics.** Assifact now systematically excludes tax credit volumes when reporting growth, noting these operations are "in esaurimento" (winding down). Including tax credits, 2025 factoring growth was only +0.18% versus +3.83% excluding them, confirming the distorting effect. The peak volume impact occurred in 2022 when reported turnover hit €287 billion with +14.5% growth — much of which was tax credit-driven.

**Key providers:** Poste Italiane, Intesa Sanpaolo, UniCredit, BNL/BNP Paribas, BPER, MPS, Enel X, Banca Ifis, Banca CF+.

## 14. Securitization provides factors with capital markets refinancing

**Italian legal term:** *Cartolarizzazione dei crediti*
**Legal basis:** Legge 130/1999 (as amended by D.L. 145/2013, D.L. 91/2014, L. 178/2020, and the March 2026 SMEs Bill)

Italian factors securitize portfolios of purchased receivables to free regulatory capital and diversify funding sources. Italy's Law 130/1999 is widely regarded as providing **the most flexible SPV regime** among the four major European markets (Italy, Germany, France, Spain).

**SPV structure.** Under Art. 3 of Law 130/1999, the SPV (*società veicolo* or *società di cartolarizzazione*) must be constituted as a *società di capitali* with the exclusive corporate purpose of carrying out securitization operations. Receivables acquired by the SPV, related collections, and financial assets purchased with them constitute a **segregated patrimony** (*patrimonio separato*) — ring-fenced from the SPV's own assets and from other securitization operations. No actions from creditors other than noteholders/lenders are permitted against the segregated assets. This segregation extends to bank accounts holding collections.

**Tranching and notes.** Italian securitizations typically feature senior (Class A, rated investment grade), mezzanine (Class B, sometimes rated), and junior/equity (Class C/J, first-loss position, often retained by the originator and unrated). Credit enhancement comes through subordination, excess spread, cash reserves, and over-collateralization.

**The servicer role.** Under Art. 2 of Law 130/1999, the master servicer must be a bank or Art. 106 TUB-registered financial intermediary, responsible for verifying conformity with law, collecting securitized receivables, and managing cash. The originating factor — such as Banca Ifis — typically continues managing receivables as servicer, maintaining the client relationship unchanged.

**Banca Ifis example.** The "IFIS ABCP Programme," initially launched in 2016 and restructured multiple times (most recently in 2024 to **€1.15 billion**), involves revolving assignment of trade receivables from ordinary factoring activities. Co-arrangers include Intesa Sanpaolo, UniCredit, BNP Paribas, Santander, Crédit Agricole, Société Générale, CDP, and Natixis.

**Recent legislative evolution.** Law 178/2020 extended SPV funding from ABS-only to include direct loans from authorized lenders — a major flexibilization. The March 2026 SMEs Bill further expanded structuring options, allowing foreign originators, multi-jurisdiction asset pools, and segregation of raw materials for inventory financing. For trade receivables specifically, D.L. 91/2014 permitted non-block (*cherry-picking*) assignment and simplified enforceability formalities using L. 52/1991 provisions rather than *Gazzetta Ufficiale* publication.

**Capital and regulatory benefits.** Securitization enables factors to achieve true sale and off-balance-sheet treatment (if risk transfer conditions are met), reducing risk-weighted assets and freeing capital for new lending. The EU Securitization Regulation (2017/2402) distinguishes STS (Simple, Transparent, Standardised) securitizations, which receive preferential capital treatment. ABS may be listed on Borsa Italiana's ExtraMOT PRO3 and receive favorable withholding tax treatment under D.Lgs. 239/1996.

## 15. B2B BNPL digitizes trade credit at the point of sale

**Italian legal term:** *Pagamento rateale B2B* / *dilazione di pagamento digitale*
**Legal basis:** General commercial law; no specific B2B BNPL legislation in Italy; IVASS for credit insurance components; PSD2 for Open Banking integrations

B2B Buy Now Pay Later is Italy's newest invoice finance product, digitizing traditional trade credit (*dilazione di pagamento*) into a point-of-sale-integrated service. The European B2B online transaction market is projected to reach **$11.26 trillion by 2030** (CAGR 22.3%), and Italy is among the early movers.

**Opyn Pay Later.** Opyn (formerly BorsadelCredito.it, founded 2012, over €2 billion cumulative receivables managed) integrates its BNPL solution into merchants' sales processes — e-commerce plugins, payment links for in-store/remote sales. The buyer selects installment or deferred payment at checkout. Opyn performs instant AI/ML credit assessment (including PSD2-enabled bank account analysis). If approved, the order is confirmed immediately. The merchant receives payment; the buyer pays Opyn in installments **without interest or spending limits**. Merchants must have annual turnover exceeding €6 million and B2B customers. Since January 2024, Opyn is available across the EU, UK, and Switzerland.

**PausePay (by CashInvoice/Allianz Trade).** Italy's first B2B BNPL solution (launched 2022 by Luca Scali). The buyer at B2B e-commerce checkout selects "Pay in 90 Days." PSD2-compliant Open Banking integration performs real-time eligibility checks underwritten by **Allianz Trade** credit insurance. If eligible, the merchant receives immediate payment; the buyer pays in 90 days. PausePay also offers an in-store option, used by Hilti Italy for construction equipment buyers, and recently signed a Visa partnership for business credit card integration.

**How B2B BNPL differs from factoring.** BNPL is embedded at the moment of purchase rather than applied retroactively to existing invoices. Credit decisioning is instant (seconds) rather than requiring lengthy factoring approval. Each transaction is independent rather than portfolio-based. It is designed for e-commerce and omnichannel integration with real-time API architecture.

**Key risks.** Regulatory uncertainty as the EU Consumer Credit Directive focuses on B2C; B2B BNPL occupies a less-regulated space. Credit assessment quality for instant decisions. Platform sustainability — PausePay planned a €1 million+ seed round for late 2024/early 2025.

## 16. Embedded finance transforms e-invoicing into a lending channel

**Italian legal term:** *Finanza incorporata* / *Lending-as-a-Service*
**Legal basis:** General commercial and financial law; Art. 106 TUB for regulated lending activities; PSD2 for Open Banking; D.Lgs. 231/2007 for AML

Italy's mandatory e-invoicing infrastructure — processing **over 2 billion invoices per year** from 3.9+ million enterprises — creates the world's most fertile ground for embedded invoice finance. Financial services are increasingly integrated directly into accounting software, ERP systems, and e-invoicing platforms.

**Workinvoice/YouDOX "AnticipaMI" model.** YouDOX is an e-invoicing and digital preservation platform operated by DocuMI, used by **over 275,000 Italian businesses** for mandatory SDI compliance. The integration with Workinvoice embeds invoice trading directly into YouDOX's dashboard: a business using YouDOX for e-invoicing can view outstanding receivables and select invoices for advance payment within the same interface. Workinvoice performs credit assessment, matches invoices with institutional investors, and delivers advance payment (typically 90%) within 48 hours. The entire flow — e-invoicing → receivable management → financing — occurs in a single integrated dashboard. Additional embedded partnerships include Cribis Cash (CRIF's platform serving 1.7+ million companies), Azimut Marketplace, Banca Sella, Sparkasse, and Enel X.

**FinDynamic's multi-bank integration.** FinDynamic's platform integrates with client ERPs and with portfolio management systems of **multiple banks simultaneously** through a single interface. Each client company chooses its banking partners; FinDynamic provides uniform digital processes across all. The Credemtel GAWDD connector links Credemtel's e-invoicing service directly to FinDynamic, creating automatic invoice-to-dynamic-discounting workflows. Over **15,000 suppliers** and 100+ buyers are active on the platform.

**Credimi/Banca CF+ API model.** Credimi's original approach (2015–2022) was API-first: SMEs applied online in 2 minutes for invoice financing; Credimi purchased invoices directly and securitized them through SPVs (Lumen SRL, Perseveranza SPA), issuing notes sold to banks (Intesa Sanpaolo, Banco BPM, Banca Sella, Deutsche Bank) and institutional investors (Tikehau Capital, Golden Tree). The platform intermediated over **€2.2 billion** total. After financial difficulties (monthly fixed costs of ~€800,000 proved unsustainable as volumes fell), the business was acquired by Banca CF+ for €5.5 million plus €4.5 million earn-out. Banca CF+ now operates "Digital Lending" — unsecured loans of €20,000–€500,000 for SMEs, with eligibility assessed in 3 minutes, fully digital.

**Opyn Universe (Lending-as-a-Service).** Opyn's proprietary platform is sold as white-label LaaS to banks, asset managers, and corporates. It covers the entire lending value chain with modular, configurable products (BNPL, term lending, invoice finance), AI/ML-powered credit assessment including PSD2 account analysis, and automated processes with human oversight for complex decisions.

**Other embedded models.** Incassa Subito (TeamSystem + Banco BPM) embeds invoice financing within TeamSystem's ERP/accounting software ecosystem. Anticipay (Zucchetti Group) integrates into Zucchetti's management software.

**The SDI advantage.** Italy's mandatory e-invoicing creates a universal data layer enabling: real-time receivable visibility (platforms see the exact status of every invoice), automated fraud prevention (SDI transit serves as proof of invoice authenticity), enriched credit scoring (SDI data combined with PSD2 bank data and CRIF/Cerved bureau data), and automated trigger flows (invoice creation → SDI transit → automatic financing offer within the same software). No other European country has this infrastructure at scale, giving Italian embedded finance platforms a structural advantage.

**Key risks.** Technology integration requires continuous maintenance. Embedded models depend on distribution partners whose strategic priorities may shift. Regulatory uncertainty looms as regulators may impose new requirements on platforms operating in grey areas. The failure of Credimi (balance-sheet model) versus the survival of Workinvoice and FinDynamic (marketplace/platform models) demonstrates that capital-light architectures are essential for sustainability.

## The regulatory architecture that holds it all together

Italy's factoring legal framework rests on three pillars. **Legge 52/1991** is the dedicated factoring statute, but it does not create a comprehensive regulatory regime — it merely modifies the general Civil Code assignment regime for qualified transactions. Under Art. 1, the law applies only when the assignor is an entrepreneur, the receivables arise from business contracts, and the assignee is a bank or registered financial intermediary. Its most important innovation is Art. 5's alternative enforceability mechanism: if the factor pays consideration with *data certa*, the assignment is enforceable against third parties without debtor notification. Art. 4 reverses the Civil Code default — the assignor **guarantees debtor solvency** unless the factor expressly waives it (creating pro soluto), whereas the Codice Civile Art. 1267 provides no solvency guarantee unless expressly agreed.

**The Codice Civile** (Arts. 1260–1267) provides the underlying framework. Art. 1260 establishes that credits can be assigned without debtor consent. Art. 1264 governs debtor notification effects. Art. 1265 resolves priority conflicts among multiple assignees (first notification with certain date prevails). These provisions apply to all receivable assignments, including those that don't meet L. 52/1991's qualifying criteria.

**The TUB** (D.Lgs. 385/1993) Art. 106 requires that entities professionally engaged in granting financing to the public register in the Albo degli intermediari finanziari maintained by Banca d'Italia. Since 2016, a single register replaced the old dual-register system. Registration takes **6–12 months** and requires approximately €2 million in equity. Non-registered entities can acquire receivables under general Civil Code rules but cannot benefit from L. 52/1991's protections and cannot engage in professional financing activity.

Assifact, the Italian Factoring Association (founded 1988), represents substantially the entire market with **45 members** (32 ordinary/corresponding financial intermediaries plus 13 supporting members). It serves as Italy's representative within the EU Federation for the Factoring and Commercial Finance Industry (EUF) and operates the CrediFact observatory for market statistics.

## Conclusion: five forces shaping Italy's factoring future

Italy's factoring ecosystem is at an inflection point driven by five converging forces. First, **the e-invoicing data layer** provides a structural advantage unmatched in Europe — 2+ billion government-validated invoices annually create the foundation for embedded finance, real-time credit scoring, and anti-fraud systems. Second, **the wind-down of Superbonus tax credits** (€219 billion in transfers from 2020–2024) is removing an artificial volume inflator, revealing underlying organic growth of ~4% annually. Third, **ESG-linked supply chain finance** is moving from pilot to mainstream, with programs like Fincantieri's and ENI's tying financing terms to supplier sustainability ratings. Fourth, **the consolidation of specialist players** — Banca CF+'s acquisition of both Credimi and Banca Sistema creates a dominant entity in digital lending and PA receivables. Fifth, **embedded finance and B2B BNPL** represent the next growth frontier, transforming invoice financing from a back-office treasury function into a point-of-sale capability integrated directly into procurement and e-commerce workflows. With Italian companies still carrying **€500 billion in unpaid invoices** at any given time and average payment periods far exceeding European norms, the market's structural demand driver remains as potent as ever.
